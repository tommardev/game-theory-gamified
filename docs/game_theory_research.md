# Game Theory: A Short-Mid Overview

Game theory is a branch of mathematics and economics that studies **strategic decision-making** in situations where the outcome for each participant (or "player") depends not only on their own actions but also on the actions of others.

## Core Concepts

- **Players:** The individuals, groups, or entities making decisions.
- **Strategies:** The set of all possible plans or actions available to each player.
- **Payoffs:** The rewards, losses, or outcomes resulting from the combination of strategies chosen by all players.
- **Nash Equilibrium:** A central solution concept where no player can improve their own outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming the other players' strategies remain unchanged.

## Key Frameworks

- **Cooperative vs. Non-Cooperative Games:** This distinguishes between games where players can form binding agreements (cooperative) and those where they cannot (non-cooperative).
- **Zero-Sum Games:** A situation where one player's gain is exactly equal to the other player's loss (e.g., poker or chess). Non-zero-sum games are those where both players can win or lose.
- **Sequential vs. Simultaneous Games:** In sequential games, players take turns, and later players have some knowledge of earlier actions. In simultaneous games, players act at the same time without knowing others' moves.

## Origins and Applications

- **History:** The field emerged in the 1920s with the work of John von Neumann, notably his Minimax Theorem, and was further formalized in the 1944 book _Theory of Games and Economic Behavior_ by von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern. John Nash later made significant contributions to the field.
- **Usage:** Game theory is used to analyze conflict, competition, and cooperation across diverse fields including **economics, business strategy, biology (evolutionary game theory), political science, and computer science**.

By providing a structured language to model interdependent decision-making, game theory helps strategists and researchers predict behavior and design better outcomes in complex competitive environments.
